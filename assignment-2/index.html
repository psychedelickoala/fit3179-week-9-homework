Australia's Changing Climate

Since the industrial revolution in the 18th and 19th centuries, many countries have become increasingly reliant on fossil fuels for energy. This has allowed us to make advances in technology and human comfort. However, burning fossil fuels releases greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere. These gases stay in the atmosphere and absorb heat which would otherwise escape the earth, contributing to global warming. The global temperature is expected to rise between 2 and 4 degrees Celsius by 2100, a significant and rapid change which could have devastating effects.

In response to this issue, a few international initiatives have been proposed to limit greenhouse gas emissions and slow climate change. One initiative is the Kyoto protocol, which entered into force in February 2005 and set emission reduction targets for 37 industrialised countries, including Australia and the US. Another is the Paris agreement, which came into force in 2016.

The plot on the left shows that Australia's CO2 emissions per capita is high compared to other countries, being over three times the global average. Since the implementation of these global agreements, though, our emissions have been decreasing.

Temperature change caused by global warming can be observed in historical data. The graph on the right allows for exploration of average daily minimum and maximum temperature over time. The trend of minimum and maximum temperatures rising can be observed in all months, but is particularly egregious in summer.

Take January as an example. We see that the minimum daily temperature is around 12 or 13 degrees Celsius from 1945-1950, but from 2010 onwards is always over 14°C. From 2005 onwards, the average maximum temperature is over 26°C every year except 2011 and 2021. However, from 1944-1960, this only occurs around half the time.

There are several outliers in this small sample. However, the trend of maximum and minimum temperatures rising carries across most months.
